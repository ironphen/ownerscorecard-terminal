*Working notes — outline to be written in R.G.'s voice.*

- Open with the constraint, stated plainly: a small investor can be exactly right
  about a business and barely compensated for it. When positions are small, a
  20–30% move in the underlying pays a dinner out, not the thesis. Being right
  and being paid are different events.
- Name the problem precisely: **the translation problem.** Graham's framework tells
  you what is mispriced; it says nothing about how to be compensated when position
  sizes are tiny. Options are one expression layer — they translate analytical
  edge into outcomes that matter, and the price of the translation is a deadline.
  The weighing machine, on a clock.
- Honesty section: Buffett faced the same constraint ("more ideas than money" in
  the partnership years) and his answer was different — concentration and obscure
  tiny stocks (the "50% a year on $1 million" claim was about universe, not
  leverage; the 2004 Korean stocks bought from a paper manual). Convexity is a
  different answer to the same constraint. Don't pretend the masters blessed it.
- The cost, stated without flinching: in a convex book the drawdown and the spike
  are often the same positions mid-flight, and the deadline can turn a correct
  thesis into a worthless one. Whoever runs this approach owes the reader that
  sentence early.
- Close by pointing at the doctrine page: the way out isn't abstinence, it's a
  dial (see "The Sizing Doctrine").

*Working notes — outline to be written in R.G.'s voice.*

- Setup: the index fund is the defensive investor's solved problem (see "1976").
  This essay asks the uncomfortable sequel: does it still meet Graham's own
  defensive criteria?
- The arithmetic (verify current figures at publication; cite S&P/index sources):
  top-10 weight of the S&P 500 has roughly doubled in a decade to around 40%.
  The "diversified" vehicle is, mechanically, a concentrated momentum bet on a
  handful of correlated mega-caps — concentration that arrived without anyone
  choosing it.
- The mechanism to teach: cap-weighting buys more of whatever just went up,
  automatically, with every paycheck. Diversification by *count* (500 names) vs
  diversification by *weight* (the only kind that matters in a drawdown).
- Historical base rates: what happened to investors who held the top ten of any
  era for the following decade (the Nifty Fifty, 1989 Japan within global
  indices, 2000's top ten). The top of the index has historically been the
  worst-performing neighborhood in it.
- Per the doctrine: levels, not schedules. No prediction that concentration
  breaks — only what the defensive investor can *do* about a defensive product
  that drifted: equal-weight sleeves, ex-US allocation, the 25–75 flex Graham
  actually prescribed. Questions, not verdicts.
- This is also the quiet companion to the enterprising symbiosis thesis: the
  same flows that concentrate the top abandon the bottom.

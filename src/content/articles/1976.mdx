*Working notes — outline to be written in R.G.'s voice.*

- The fact-cluster the whole essay hangs on: in 1976 Benjamin Graham gave his
  final interview (Financial Analysts Journal) and said he was no longer an
  advocate of elaborate security analysis for finding superior values — too many
  smart people doing it for it to pay the average practitioner. Vanguard's First
  Index Investment Trust — the first retail index fund — launched that August.
  Graham died in September. One year: the author concedes, the product ships,
  the era turns. (Verify the interview quote against the original before
  publishing.)
- The claim: the *defensive* half of The Intelligent Investor has been solved
  and productized. Automated payroll flows into a broad index are Graham's
  defensive prescription executed better than he specified it — wide
  diversification, low cost, no decisions, dollar-cost averaging by default.
- The dignity point (this is the voice of the site): in Graham's frame, indexing
  is not the consolation prize for people who can't pick stocks. It is the
  correct answer for people who priced their own time honestly. The defensive
  investor didn't fail; they finished.
- Bridge to the sequel essays: what remains unsolved is the enterprising path
  ("The Two Investors, 2026") and whether the defensive product still satisfies
  its own definition ("Is the Index Still Defensive?").

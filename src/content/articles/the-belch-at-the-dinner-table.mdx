*Working notes — outline to be written in R.G.'s voice.*

- The setup, with receipts: Buffett and Munger spent decades condemning
  executive compensation in letters and at meetings — while Berkshire HQ kept
  famously modest pay (the $100k salary). The hypocrisy isn't at home; it's in
  the boardrooms: Buffett sat on boards (Coca-Cola, most visibly in 2014) where
  he *abstained* rather than vote against packages he privately disliked — "you
  don't belch at the dinner table," by his own admission. Verify the episode
  details before publishing.
- The original contribution — the question one size too small: the comp debate
  has only ever asked whether the CEO is overpaid *relative to shareholders*.
  Nobody at any of those tables asked the larger one: overpaid relative to the
  *payroll*. What about the rest of the company — and the teachers, nurses, and
  entry-level employees who can verify the proxy numbers but not afford to move
  out?
- The teachable mechanic (this becomes the standing feature "proxy season for
  the rest of us"): how to find total comp in any proxy statement, set it
  against operating income and against total payroll where disclosed (the pay
  ratio rule gives median employee comp). The $4M-out-of-$40M-operating-income
  comparison, computed live on a real company, step by step.
- Doctrine check: incentive structures, not villains. The board mechanics —
  comp consultants, peer benchmarking ratchets ("nobody's CEO is below
  average"), collegiality pressure — explain the outcome without requiring a
  single bad person. That's what makes it durable.
- Tone guard: this piece carries the site's anger at maximum load. The
  arithmetic must do all of the shouting.

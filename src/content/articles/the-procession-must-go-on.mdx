*Working notes — outline to be written in R.G.'s voice. Read the actual tale
first (Hans Christian Andersen, 1837 — three pages).*

- What's actually in the story: the swindlers announce the cloth is invisible to
  anyone *unfit for office or hopelessly stupid*. That sentence is the whole
  machine. The minister sees nothing and lies to protect his office. The emperor
  sees nothing and lies for the same reason. No villain, no fool — only an
  incentive structure where doubt equals self-incrimination. Andersen criticizes
  no one; the structure does all the work. (This is the site's Carnegie standard
  executed by a fairy tale, which is why it survived 200 years.)
- The mapping that passes the three-question test — the admission price, reissued
  every era: "you just don't understand the new economy" (1999); "you don't
  understand structured credit" (2007); "have fun staying poor" (2021); "you
  don't get the mission" (this morning, at $135 a share). The transferable
  skill: **when the rebuttal to your doubt is that doubting proves you unfit to
  judge, you are looking at the cloth.**
- The minister is career risk: Keynes — better for reputation to fail
  conventionally than to succeed unconventionally. The analyst can't downgrade
  what his bank underwrote; the manager who's early gets redeemed before he gets
  proven right.
- The child isn't smarter — the child is *unconflicted*. No office, no AUM, no
  benchmark. That has always been the retail investor's one structural edge:
  the freedom to not pretend.
- The gut-punch turn: Andersen's crowd cheered for free. This week the crowd
  with the child's freedom paid $70 billion to join the procession (link "The
  $70 Billion Bid").
- The forgotten ending — and the title: after the child shouts and the whole
  crowd takes up the cry, the Emperor suspects they are right and decides the
  procession must go on, walking prouder than ever while the noblemen carry the
  train that isn't there. Truth being spoken doesn't stop the parade — the
  shout and the top are routinely years apart, because the chamberlains' jobs
  are holding the train. The essay teaches the no-top-calling discipline from
  inside the story: the child was right, and the parade continued anyway.
- The closing symmetry, kept for ourselves as much as the reader: there are two
  ways to lose your eyesight — needing to see the cloth, and needing to *not*
  see it. The crown is a role, not a man; on our worst days it fits us too.

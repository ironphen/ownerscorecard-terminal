# The Qualitative Doctrine

*What a business read must deliver, and the discipline it is held to.*

This is the governing spec for the qualitative copy on every company page — the
"what it is", "what moves the needle", and "is it a good business" reads. It is
drawn from Benjamin Graham (*Security Analysis*, *The Intelligent Investor*) and
from Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger as they reinforced and modernized him over
fifty years of Berkshire Hathaway letters. Every word of seeded qualitative copy
answers to it.

The product's promise is to be the canonical free place a serious investor sends a
beginner to learn to read a business the way these men do. The numbers are the
spine; this doctrine is how we say what the numbers are *about* without ever
telling the reader what to conclude.

---

## First principles — the spirit before the rules

**1. Buy the business, not the ticker.** Every read is written for someone deciding
whether to own the whole company. *"Investment is most intelligent when it is most
businesslike"* (Graham, *The Intelligent Investor*, ch. 20). The page is an owner's
walk-through, in the order an owner asks: what is it, what has it done, will it
survive and is it run for me, and only then — what would I pay.

**2. Present, never pronounce.** Graham's deepest warning is that qualitative
factors invite overpayment: the better the story, the more people pay for it, and
the less margin of safety is left. So we *describe* the business and hand the
reader the lens; we never deliver a verdict, a score, or a recommendation. The
judgment is the reader's, always. This is not modesty — it is the discipline that
keeps the product honest.

**3. A business is what it has done across a cycle.** Graham's normalized
earning power — averaged over seven to ten years — not last quarter. We weigh the
worst year as heavily as the best, because that is where survival is decided.

**4. The number is sacred; the prose is only the lens.** The model that drafts
this copy may characterize *what kind* of business this is and *what moves* it. It
may never author, restate, or round a figure. Every number on the page is
arithmetic on the filings, shown in full. This is why **"Is it a good business?"
stays computed** — it cites the real return on capital, the real owner earnings,
the real cash-conversion cycle, and a drafted sentence has no business touching
those.

## The owner's questions, in order

### 1. What is it? — the circle of competence

Buffett's first filter is a business you can understand. The read says, in plain
English, what the company sells, to whom, and how it makes money — concretely
enough that a beginner could explain it back. No mission statements, no
aspiration, no jargon. Drawn from the company's own filing where it has one; from
the numbers and the company's established identity where the filing text is thin
(see *Grounding*, below). If a business cannot be said simply, that difficulty is
itself the finding.

### 2. What kind of business is it? — franchise or commodity

The central qualitative distinction in the whole canon. Buffett, 1991: *an economic
franchise arises from a product or service that (1) is needed or desired, (2) is
thought by its customers to have no close substitute, and (3) is not subject to
price regulation.* A franchise can raise prices and earn high returns; a commodity
business is a price-taker that lives or dies on being the low-cost producer.

Pricing power is the sharpest single test. Buffett to the Financial Crisis Inquiry
Commission, 2010: *"The single most important decision in evaluating a business is
pricing power. If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a
competitor, you've got a very good business. And if you have to have a prayer
session before raising the price by 10 percent, then you've got a terrible
business."*

The read names which kind this looks like and points to where the evidence would
show — gross margin held through inflation, share kept when a cheaper rival
appears — but it never *asserts* a moat. Whether the advantage is real is what the
filing and the record are for.

### 3. What moves the needle? — the one or two real variables

Every business turns on a few things. The read isolates them and binds them to
*this* company's economics, with the bad case kept in view. The *lens* is the
industry's — a software business turns on retention and the cost of growth; a
utility on the return it is allowed on its rate base; an oil producer on a price
it does not set — but the *specifics* are this company's. This is where a generic
template fails worst, so it is where grounding in the company's own situation
matters most.

### 4. Is it a good business? — return on capital, read with suspicion

Buffett, 1979: *"The primary test of managerial economic performance is the
achievement of a high earnings rate on equity capital employed (without undue
leverage, accounting gimmickry, etc.)."* Quality is high, durable returns on
capital — read with the skepticism modern accounting demands, because buybacks and
expensed R&D and brands shrink the capital base and flatter the ratio. So we lean
on the harder-to-game cash measure: owner earnings.

And the deeper distinction, Buffett 2007: a **truly great** business earns high
returns *and can reinvest* at those returns — the compounding machine. A merely
**good** one earns high returns but cannot redeploy much, so it should return its
cash (See's Candy, the "dream business"). A **gruesome** one grows only by
consuming capital at low returns. *"A truly great business must have an enduring
'moat' that protects excellent returns on invested capital. The dynamics of
capitalism guarantee that competitors will repeatedly assault any business 'castle'
that is earning high returns."*

**This section stays computed.** The prose around it only frames the figures the
arithmetic produced; it never supplies them.

### 5. What could go wrong? — invert

Munger: *"Invert, always invert."* The durability question — will the moat still be
here in ten years — and the caution that *"turnarounds seldom turn"* (Buffett). The
read names the principal risk in the company's own terms: its filing's own
emphasis where we have it, the cycle and the balance sheet where we don't.

## Owner earnings — the definition we hold to

Buffett, 1986. Owner earnings are *(a) reported earnings plus (b) depreciation,
depletion, amortization, and certain other non-cash charges … less (c) the average
annual amount of capitalized expenditures for plant and equipment, etc. that the
business requires to fully maintain its long-term competitive position and its unit
volume.* His own coda is the spirit of the entire product: this number *"does not
lend itself to the easy precision"* of reported earnings but is *"more useful …
approximately right rather than precisely wrong."* Our maintenance-capex proxy is
exactly that trade — an honest approximation over a false precision.

## The voice

Write like the Berkshire letters: plain, concrete, unhurried, faintly wry, never
promotional. Short words. No adjective doing the work a number should do. The
reader should finish a sentence feeling *taught*, not *sold*. When in doubt, say
less and point to the arithmetic below.

---

## Durable, not current — the layer must hold for years

The qualitative layer describes the **enduring economic character** of a business —
the kind of machine it is and the levers that move it — not its current readout. A
franchise's nature changes slowly; that is exactly the layer that holds for years,
and it is the layer a value investor is actually assessing. So every note is written
to stay true, *unchanged*, for the same company across many years and many filings.

This is stricter than "no figures." It also rules out:

- **Temporal markers** — "now", "today", "currently", "recently", "this year",
  "lately". The note carries no date stamp; nothing in it should imply one.
- **Trajectory and current-state claims** — "growing its base", "margins widening",
  "cash building", "has climbed". These describe which way the needle happens to be
  pointing, and the needle reverses. Keep the *lever*; drop the *direction*.
  ("Operating leverage as the base scales" is durable; "margins have widened as the
  base grew" is not.)

Where the reader needs the current state — the actual margin, the actual member
count, this year's owner earnings — the note **points to the record below**, where
the arithmetic lives and is refreshed every cycle. The prose names what to look at;
the table says where it stands. This is also why a reviewed note is refreshed only
when a filing changes the *description or the business mix* — not every cycle —
because nothing in it is tied to a single year's numbers.

## Grounding — how the copy stays honest

The seed copy is drafted only from:

1. **The company's own filing text**, where it exists (US/ADR filers carry a
   description and risk language; this is the primary source).
2. **The computed numbers**, always — but the draft names the *lever* they bear on
   ("how far content spend stretches as the base scales"), never the figure, and
   never its *direction* (see *Durable, not current*, above). The number, and which
   way it is moving, both live in the record below.
3. **Established, non-controversial identity facts** where the filing text is thin
   — most importantly the Japanese (EDINET) names, which carry no description in our
   data. Saying "Capcom owns the Resident Evil and Monster Hunter franchises" is
   encyclopedic, not a claim under dispute. Inventing a specific the data does not
   support is forbidden.

A characterization that is consistent with public knowledge and the numbers but is
not in a filing — "the catalog is the quiet engine" — is allowed only because every
note is **reviewed before it ships**. The computed template remains the fallback
for any name without a reviewed note, so nothing renders unreviewed by accident.

---

## Hard constraints — non-negotiable

- The model drafts only the two **descriptive** sections — "what it is" and "what
  moves the needle". **"Is it a good business?" stays computed** from the record.
- The model **never writes a figure.** Numbers come from the arithmetic.
- The copy describes **enduring economic character, never current state**: no
  figures, no temporal markers, no trajectory claims. It must hold true, unchanged,
  for years (see *Durable, not current*).
- Every note is **reviewed before commit** (`reviewed: true` in `notes.json`). An
  unreviewed draft does not render.
- The **runtime stays deterministic and free.** Notes are static JSON, generated
  once offline, committed to the repo, and read at build exactly like any other
  data. No model runs in the build, and no API key is required to build the site.
- The **labeling stays honest.** On a page carrying a reviewed note, the brief says
  the lens was *drafted from the company's own filings and reviewed* and that every
  number is arithmetic. The old "no model wrote a word of this" claim is retired on
  those pages — it is kept only where it remains literally true (the filing
  text-analysis reads: owner's letter, candor, owner notice).
